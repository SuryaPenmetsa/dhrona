## Concept naming — calibration rules

Good concept names are 2–6 words that identify a single teachable idea at the right granularity.

| Too broad (avoid) | Good granularity (use) | Too narrow (avoid) |
|--------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| Algebra | Solving Linear Equations | Step 3 of the worksheet |
| Biology | Ecosystem Interactions | The frog in the diagram |
| Grammar | French Future Tense | Conjugation of "aller" |
| Art | Perspective Drawing | Shading the cube |

Rules:
- Name the idea a teacher would write on a lesson plan, not a chapter title and not a micro-step.
- Use title case: "Quadratic Equations", not "quadratic equations".
- Do not include the subject in the concept name: "Future Tense", not "French Future Tense"
  (the subject field handles that).
- Prefer noun phrases over verbs: "Quadratic Factorisation", not "Factorising Quadratics".

## Relationship taxonomy — use ONLY these labels

- "prerequisite for"
- "builds on"
- "applies"
- "extends"
- "next in school syllabus"
- "follows in schedule"
- "real-world example of"
- "same mathematical structure as"
- "contrasts with"
- "part of"
- "relates to IB key concept"
- "cross-subject link"
- "concludes unit"

Pick the closest match. Do NOT invent new relationship labels.

Guidance:
- "prerequisite for": A must be understood before B makes sense.
- "builds on": B extends A but A is not strictly required.
- "applies": B uses A in a different domain or context.
- "extends": B goes deeper into the same idea as A.
- "next in school syllabus": B follows A in the school's planned sequence.
- "follows in schedule": B comes after A in time but is not a prerequisite.
- "real-world example of": B is a concrete or applied instance of abstract A.
- "same mathematical structure as": A and B share the same formal pattern across domains.
- "contrasts with": A and B are often confused or intentionally compared.
- "part of": A is a component or subtopic of B.
- "relates to IB key concept": connects a topic concept to an IB MYP key concept.
- "cross-subject link": an explicit bridge between two different subjects.
- "concludes unit": A is the final topic of a syllabus unit.

## Edge direction — concept_a → concept_b

Direction always means: concept_a LEADS TO concept_b.
- For "prerequisite for": A is the prerequisite, B is what requires it.
- For "builds on": A is the foundation, B is what builds on it.
- For "next in school syllabus": A is current, B is upcoming.
- For "relates to IB key concept": A is the topic concept, B is the IB key concept.
- For "part of": A is the part, B is the whole.

Connect a topic concept to an IB key concept ONLY when the relationship is pedagogically
meaningful — i.e., a teacher would use that key concept to frame a unit or lesson around this topic.

Limit: at most 2 IB key concept connections per topic concept.
When connecting, set concept_b to the IB key concept name and subject_b to null.
